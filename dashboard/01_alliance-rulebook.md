# The Objective
The objective of this game is for all of the players to achieve world peace by bringing their nation-teams’ many conflicting geopolitical agendas to some sort of resolution without being at war with one another within 4 hours or some predetermined set of sessions. Then, using their experience in the game, they can have a robust discussion about the challenges of being a world leader. 

# How many players can play? 
3-15 Players or 15-72 players. With the additional teams and bigger map you can have as many as 72 players.  

20-45 minutes per session (includes at least 1 Game Day)

6-8 Sessions. So depending on how many players playing how many Game Days, and how long you want to play, it could take anywhere from 2 hours to 6 hours. Typically we aim for a 4 hour event if we do it all in one day which includes setup and clean up. 

## This is a Political Science Simulation
This is a game about trade, forming alliances, and negotiation. There are no rules for trade, forming alliances, and negotiating. 

This is roleplay. It only looks like a board game. The rules and the components are only there to give real consequences to your choices. The choices are the game. So feel free to change the rules to better support the roleplay, or make them up when necessary. 

This is a world peace themed wargame that uses the same mechanisms as traditional military wargaming. I once hosted the game together with retired military professionals who run official war game simulations for the state. Four star generals have participated in their wargames. They liked this game because it made official state wargaming accessible to a broader audience.

The U.S. government uses games like this to explore and reveal all of the possible outcomes of a hypothetical situation like war between superpowers. This game does the same. 

# Quick Setup and Immediate Play
The core of this game is a simple combination of roleplay choices and fate. Players simply choose a character, faction, or political entity and describe what kind of action they take on that role’s behalf. Then they roll a one-sided die or draw from the Fate deck of six cards to see how surprisingly well or disastrous the consequences are. Players can also discuss the details of how these consequences play out narratively and change the status of any of the various roles in the game.
So at the very minimum all you really need to play is
Some index cards to keep track of items, status, abilities, or assets generated in the game
A copy of the various different characters/roles/factions with their unique conflicting agendas
one six-sided die to simulate uncertainty
and a copy of the 6 possible Fate Card Results
Everything else, including the map and the other decks, are all optional!
The players are collaboratively creating a world in which each has agency over the different actors/roles/factions within that fictional world. By initiating Actions, making choices about what their chosen character/role/faction chooses to do, discussing how those choices play out, and introducing a bit of randomness, they can simulate any event from history, literature, or possible future.
The rest of the rules are unnecessary unless you want a more structured or thematic experience. If your players are creative and open-minded enough then you can begin playing with what has already been explained so far. 
Simply print out one copy of each National Agenda, deal them out to players, print the Fate Deck, and keep a six-sided die to begin. 
# Full Setup
For more structured and thematic play or a more guided experience setup the following components.
### Draw National Agendas
Every player draws or chooses a National Agenda card. Do NOT read the backs of the cards (The Secret Objectives) until everyone has made their choice.
The National Agenda card includes their team’s starting resources, their place on the Status Tracker, and on the back of the card are their Secret Objectives.
Prepare the Board & Deck Expansions
### Prepare the Fate Deck 
Shuffle the 6 cards in the Fate Deck. Place the deck face down on the table where everyone can reach.
Drawing from this deck can be simulated with the roll of a six-sided dice. This is recommended if you don’t have professionally printed cards, if the cards are printed on paper but not cut perfectly and placed in card sleeves, or if the cards are damaged. If this is the case lay the cards out face up so players can see how the die roll corresponds to results.
### Prepare the Resource Token Piles
Place the Resource Tokens into five different piles. You can use the micro cards which contain the titles on one side and the mechanic on the other, or alternatively, you could also use an assortment of red, blue, white, black, and green poker chips if you have them. 
#### Give each Nation its Resource Tokens
Give each player Resource Tokens (either represented by micro cards or poker chips and referred to hereafter as Tokens). 
Every nation has the number of green, red, blue, black, or white Resource Tokens their nation starts with illustrated below the flag of their National Agenda. 
Each nation has a surplus in one color and a deficiency in another. These colors also correspond to the symbols on the hexagon map that their nation can produce, so their surplus is likely to increase. Players should take notice and consider this when they trade. 
### Shuffle the Market Deck
Shuffle the Market Deck and place face down near the map. Draw one card from this deck on each team’s turn to see which icon produces additional Resource Token for each team. Each team gets one Resource Token for each corresponding symbol they have on their part of the map. 
Shuffle the deck each time the purple token appears and draw again. 
### Shuffle the Secret Objectives Deck
Shuffle the Secret Objectives and set them aside. These can be drawn by players who engage in espionage, or given out at the discretion of the facilitator[s] at any time to add a little intrigue to the game. 
### Shuffle the Innovations Deck
Shuffle Innovation cards and place the deck face-down on the table. These can be drawn by players who engage in Innovation, or given out at the discretion of the facilitator[s] at any time to employ disengaged players. 
Crisis Cards deck (increases Difficulty)
### Shuffle the Crises deck. Do not use during the first round of turns. 
Starting in Round 2, have a team draw one Crisis card at the beginning of their turn. This card must be resolved by the end of the round.
Like the Secret Objectives and Innovation Decks, these can also be issued more or less often at the discretion of the facilitator[s] to increase the challenge of the game or slow down any particular team’s progress. 
## Choose One Action Per Game-Day
Each team will submit one Action Report. The Actions typically fall into one of three different categories...
Innovate Actions are critical to solving problems (builds assets that could be stolen by weaker players making this player vulnerable to attack)
Build Actions improve the active player’s Status or another player’s Status and can enrich teams by producing new assets and/or resources 
Engage Actions are aggressive actions taken against other players like Stealing, Espionage, & War
# Win/Lose Conditions
This is a story game in which players collaborate, collude, and conspire to craft a collective story. It is not a zero-sum game in which only one player can win. All players win if each team / player reaches a level of Status above the red X on the Status Tracker. If anyone is at or below the red X then everyone failed to achieve world peace. 
The Status Tracker is is merely a vanity metric unless reduced to the red X. This signifies that your constituents are unhappy with you and your party’s leadership.  
Try to play at least two rounds of turns or gamedays. You may stop the game there or continue for as long as the facilitator has scheduled time for. 
# Sessions and Game Days.
A Session describes each time players meet to play together for more than 15 minutes. A game could have one Session where players play from beginning to end, or the game could be played over multiple Sessions when players agree to meet together. 
Each Session should contain at least one Game Day in which each player/team does one major Action as written out on their Action Report.
## 1. Draw from the Market Deck. 
Before the key leaders disperse for the next game day, draw from the market deck and let each team collect 1 token of that color for each matching symbol on their map. If you draw a blue market card then every nation with a blue lightning symbol on their map collects a Blue token for each matching symbol within their border. 
## 2. Meet with your team to agree on an Action.
At the very beginning of each Game Day, players of each team/nation should discuss what Action they will take and fill out an Action Report. Players can attempt any Action they  think appropriate for the  theme of the game, but if  they struggle to come up with anything you can look at the Actions listed out in the Lists below. 
Players must submit ONLY ONE Action Report per Game Day. 
Draw a Market Card on behalf of all the teams at the beginning of each turn. If youo have more than 20 players you may want to draw two Market cards at the beginning of each Game Day.
Teams may also move a standing army once per Game Day. See “Moving Pieces on the Board” section for the rules on this.
At the beginning of each game day post the time of day when the Action Reports must be submitted by for it to be adjudicated by the end of the Game Day. 
For example, one might write “12:15pm” on a big white board and declare to the room, “Each team’s Action Report for Game Day 4 must be submitted by 12:15!”
Have One Player from Each Team Fill Out the Action Report 
While the other players run off to trade or negotiate with other teams, at least one player from each team should take responsibility for filling out the Action Report.
Depending on whether you have a Facilitator or a team of referees, once the Action Report is filled out each team can
submit their Action Report to the Facilitator, 
submit them to an impartial player to adjudicate (if there is no facilitator), 
or simply reveal them publicly for all Players to adjudicate. 
## Types of Actions
### Build
Players may  Build as their Action. This is generally done in preparation for a bigger Action to be taken on a later Game Day. They can build anything they like, but here are some suggestions. 
Militarize Secure the nation against an identified external threat
Manufacturing Infrastructure to mass implement a new technology or reproduce a new technology for commercial purposes
Prepare to make a difficult Action on the next Game Day less difficult
Contribute to other nations to improve relations and further develop one of their projects
Invest in Social Welfare Programs
Educate Invest in the future generation or research universities
Clean Up Corruption, Pollution, Drug Trafficking, etc
Police Strengthen their nation’s Internal Police Force
Lobby to raise funds from Corporations for a political agenda
Raise Taxes to raise funds for a politically popular program
Build Actions are plausible strategies within the power of leaders to improve the Status of their nation.
These actions should only typically be opposed if they go against the predescribed ideologies of the various players’ National Agendas, or if the consequences identified by the player seem too overpowering.
After choosing an action the player may write on an index card what they are building and what consequences they hope to achieve with it.
If the Build Action is unopposed 
The Action automatically succeeds. 
The player may use this card on future turns/Game Days. 
This player or another player’s Status improves +1.
#### Opposition
Another Player may oppose this action, but they must identify a plausible story of who or what obstacles this Action would face in a realistic real world scenario. In the case of an opposition both players must agree on an impartial player to adjudicate. This referee must choose either
In favor of the Player who chose to Build
Suggest an alternative set of results all three players can agree on or
Declare the Difficulty Level after weighing the Pro and Con Arguments  as either
Difficult to achieve (requires they fulfill the conditions for success on a Fate Card Draw)
Likely to succeed (Only fails if they draw a “FAIL” Fate Card)
And then Draw a Fate Card to see if he/she succeeds
or Simply let both players draw from the Fate Deck to see who draws the higher card.
### Innovate
Innovate Actions generally allow players to build technological solutions that can be drawn from the Innovations deck, written down on index cards, or represented by colored cubes. 
Any card or cube introduced during the game is called an Asset.
These technological solutions can be extremely powerful. If a player tries to achieve something extremely powerful all in one Game Day  this will be identified as “Very Difficult” and require some luck. But if you work together with other nations, spend multiple turns chaining actions together towards this one purpose, or provide detailed plausible reasons why it should be successful, then you will have a higher chance of success. 
Just keep in mind that, like any other asset or resource in the game, it is vulnerable to being sabotaged, stolen, or directly attacked by other players who choose Engage Actions.
Players may take the following Innovate actions.
Draw an Innovation card from the Innovations deck
Research an idea from popular science, or from a near-future science fiction written on a note-card.
Further Develop a current technology
Invent a new technology
Innovate by combining two previously uncombined technologies
Share technological findings with a University from another nation to see if they can further develop the technology.
After choosing an Action the team / player must fill out an AR (Action Report). 
The AR will require them to write out what they are trying to accomplish, the desired result, any tokens they are willing to gamble for it, and up to three reasons why they should be successful.
Then the facilitator or an impartial Player must read their AR and assign a Difficulty Level.
Difficulty Level can be one of the following
Very Difficult to achieve (requires they draw “Success” or “Very Successful” Fate Card)
Difficult to achieve (remove one “Risky” card from the Fate deck before drawing. Player/team must fulfill the conditions for success on a Fate Card Draw)
Likely to succeed (100% Success unless they draw a “FAIL” Fate Card) 
Easy/Reasonable (automatic success)
The facilitator may modify the odds to make the outcome more risky by removing one or two “Risky” cards or the “Opportunity,” card from the Fate Deck before having the team/player draw.
### Engage
Players may take the following Engage Actions against other players.
Invade	Steal
Sabotage	Put down unrest
Spy	

After choosing an Action they must fill out an AR (Action Report). This report will require them to write out what they are trying to accomplish, the intended results, and up to three Pro arguments for why they should be successful.
Intended Results
The “Intended Results,” could include
What if anything the engaging player will gain. This can include a technology being stolen from the targeted player or an improvement to the engaging player’s Status
Resource Tokens that will be lost or gained
What harm or damage the targeted player will suffer
Pro Arguments could reference
Previous Actions taken by players that relate to this Action	Details of this particular nation’s characteristics, people or resources
Details of the player’s allies’ characteristics, people, or resources (PCs or NPCs)	Cards, Assets, Status, or any other mechanisms of the game

The team/player they are acting against may also add Cons and consequences to the engaging player’s Action Report before it is adjudicated. 
The opposing player may include up to three Cons why they think the engaging player should fail. The opponent may also write or declare an alternate set of consequences if the engaging player fails the Fate Card draw.
The two players in opposition must then agree on which other player will adjudicate if there is no facilitator. The referee adjudicator must read the Pro and Con arguments in the Action Report and assign a Difficulty Level.
Then the Player who initiated the Action must draw a card from the Fate Deck. Based on the card drawn, the adjudicator referee will determine whether the Action fails or succeeds, and at what cost.
The adjudicator must decide 
1. Which intended results written on the Action Report occur, 
2. The details of how the story unfolds, and 
3. (Optional) add an unexpected unintended consequence so long as it seems reasonable and fits the theme.
Possible Results
Engage Actions could result in any combination of the following
A team could gain or lose 1 Status	A team could  gain or lose Resource Tokens
The Aggressing team/player could take an asset previously Built by the opposing team/player.	An invading Army could be made to retreat.
An Army that loses a war could be destroyed or reduced in capacity. 	An Index card with a consequence written on it could be issued to a team/player. 

If a team/player fails and wants to attempt the Action again
The losing player/team reduces their Status by -1 AND increases their Difficulty level to try again. 	If current Difficulty Level is at “Likely to Succeed” it increases to “Difficult,” if the team/player tries again
If current Difficulty Level is at “Difficult” then it increases to “Very Difficult,” if the team/player tries again	A player’s Status cannot be reduced below 1 but remains at 1

## 3. Trade & Negotiate
This is where it gets messy. Give players 10-14 minutes to freely roam around the room, trade, negotiate, and form alliances until the Acton Reports are due. 
After the time allotted ends you can quiet them down to adjudicate their Action Reports. 
## 4. Adjudication
Order of Adjudication 
The Actions that involve only one team/player should quickly be adjudicated first. Then Actions that are not contested by any other team/player should also quickly be adjudicated.
Lastly, the Actions that are more aggressive or contested by one or more teams should be adjudicated. 
Actions should be adjudicated by a designated facilitator, by an impartial player, or by a discussion among the most impartial players. 
Any team targeted by an aggressive Action taken by another team may be granted priority to go first in the next Round if they choose to respond with their next Action. Then the uncontested Actions involving fewer teams/players would be adjudicated for that Round.
Costs are to be determined by Adjudication
The Fate cards each have a reward and/or cost in terms of Status, but the Facilitator or players adjudicating can change this to a Token cost/reward or any other creative alternative that seems reasonable. For example, if the Action is related to Technology then the player could win or lose Blue Weapons Technology Resource Tokens as opposed to Status. 
## Optional Adjudicator Additions
The Facilitator/Adjudicator is also allowed to add their own creative unexpected consequences to the resolution of any negotiation or engagement so long as both players agree that the consequence is reasonable. 
Here is a rather sophisticated example: If a Player leads a radically conservative nation and makes a compromise with a radically liberal Player of a corrupt nation, the Refereeing player can suggest that terrorist attacks break out in the conservative nation causing it to reduce Status by -1, and the player leading the corrupt liberal nation must write down on an index card that he had to bribe senators in his own nation to keep them from stirring up trouble. This fact may be used against the bribing player in future conflicts.
## 5. End of Game Day
### Announcements, Speeches, and Votes
The Facilitator should announce results of battles, competitions, and covert operations (discerning whether to announce the results they had on the unsuspecting player but NOT revealing the culprit), allowing everyone to know who has built new Assets, whose assets have been destroyed, sabotaged, or robbed, whose strategies have been blocked (sanctions) for the next . 
Make this time as formal as possible, and make absolutely sure that everyone quiets down to hear the game status updates. This provides a much-needed structure to an otherwise chaotic game. 
### (Optional) Have one team draw from the Crisis Deck and/or the Innovation Deck.
Crises interrupt team plans and add variety to the game. It also encourages sympathy and collaboration between teams.  This can be done any time at any phase.
You can have one team draw one each game day, choose a team that is running away with the game, have them draw randomly, or choose a card based on the previous actions taken in the game. It is entirely up to the discretion of the facilitator how the referees should use these cards. The same applies to Innovation cards which can be a boon to less active teams. 
### Check the Schedule
Once all Actions have been resolved, announcements announced, and cards drawn, the facilitator must choose whether to pause the game and end the session, or begin another Game Day.
*Always remember to shuffle the Fate Deck after resolving any actions. 
Ending the Game
If you don’t have enough time to complete up to seven rounds of team s, or if you need to end the game while there are unresolved conflicts going on there are two alternative ways to end the game. 
First, declare the game finished, reveal all of the Secret Objective cards if you are using them, and update the Status Tracker.
World Peace. If no one is at war and every team is above the Red X on the Status Tracker then everyone wins!	The Shakespearian Tragedy. There is no winner if any player/team is at or below the Red X on the Status Tracker.

If time allows then have each team fill out an AR (Action Report) declaring how they think the story/game ended and up to three reasons expressing how or why.
Each player/team’s AR is assigned a Difficulty Level by the player sitting to their right. Each team draws a Fate card. If they achieve the conditions of success on their Fate card then their AR becomes part of the official story of the game. 
What’s more important than how you end the game, is to set aside time for everyone to discuss how they feel about the way the narrative unfolded. There should be some consensus about the overall narrative of the game. Let players reflect on and share their private experiences with one another. 
### Moving Pieces across the board
To move a standing army simply draw a Fate card.
Refer to the symbol at the bottom left of the card.
You may move your standing armies any number of spaces adding up to the number on the bottom left of the Fate card.
To maintain a standing army on foreign territory you must pay 1 Status each  or 1 Green and 1 Black token each round. If you cannot pay you must retreat from foreign territory and re your Standing Army cube to any hexagon in your nation’s part of the map.
As soon as two Standing Armies occupy the same hexagon, or if a Standing Army reaches a foreign capital, those two players must immediately engage in war. 
Any team may move a Standing Army once per turn or game day on any team’s turn. 
## Battles
To keep the game moving quickly battles can be adjudicated by having each side draw a Fate card. If you want to give advantage to one side based on
- which side spent more blue tokens in a silent auction (have each player place Blue Tokens in a closed fist and reveal simultaneously)
- if they have more allies
- arguments made in the pros and cons on the Action Report or
- weapons tech built on previous turns
then you can grant advantage to one side or the other depending on which side has more of the previously listed prepared. 

### Advantage
To grant advantage to one side, simply remove the 2 pips dice card titled "Risky" from the deck before that player draws a card. To grant even more advantage restore the card and remove a 5 pip "success" card before the disadvantaged player draws from the deck. 

The player who draws the higher card wins. If the difference between the dice pips illustrated on the card is 3 or more, the losing side's standing army is destroyed. 

# Tips for the Facilitator[s]
If you are organizing the game, that makes you what we refer to as the Facilitator, or what Tabletop RPG games would call a Game Master, what sports would call a referee, or what other megagames call head of the control team. 
As the facilitator you have a few different responsibilities that no other players have. 
Adjudication. You’ll have the ultimate say in adjudicating the consequences of Fate deck draws. 
Roleplay. If players want to interact with a NPC (non-playable character) then you get to roleplay as that nation/character/role/state bureaucracy.
Make New Rules. If there are no rules for an Action the players want to take that you know of, then you get to make them up on the fly!
You will also need to keep an eye on the schedule, speed up or slow down s, and manage the flow of resources in the game using the following different levers. 
Speed Ups. Dice wars can be replaced with a draw from the Fate deck.
Increase the Challenge of the Game. Have more players draw from the Crisis deck. This is also a way to reduce the amount of Tokens at play. 
Make the Game Easier. Draw from the Market deck more often, or increase the number of Tokens issued on each draw. 
Seed the Game with Technology. Issue Innovation cards or Secret Objective cards randomly or deliberately, publicly or secretly.
